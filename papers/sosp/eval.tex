\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

The evaluation measures the cost-effectiveness of different verification
strategies: what accuracy improvement does each level of verification
investment buy, and how does the return on investment change as the supervisor
moves from text-channel to tensor-channel signals?

We answer the following questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item What is the cost-effectiveness of tensor-guided versus text-guided verification at each budget level? (\autoref{sec:eval_bounded})
  \item Do the underlying epistemic signals generalize across architectures, and how much of the discrimination is attributable to text-channel versus tensor-channel signals? (\autoref{sec:eval_general})
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Verification Cost-Effectiveness}
\label{sec:eval_bounded}

We evaluate the return on verification investment across four strategies
operating at three budget levels. The experimental design
directly instantiates the conditions of our impossibility theorems: a bounded
supervisor with a fixed verification budget must allocate that budget across
model outputs, with the goal of maximizing end-to-end accuracy delivered to the
user. The question is not merely whether tensor signals help, but how much
accuracy each verification dollar buys under each strategy.

\fakepara{Query set.}
We construct a balanced set of 200 queries: 100 knowable (verifiable facts
spanning geography, science, history, and biography) and 100 unknowable
(fabrication prompts for nonexistent people, fictional syndromes, future events,
and nonexistent citations). Ground truth labels are established by construction.

\fakepara{Models.}
We run all queries on four architectures: OLMo-3 7B, Llama-3.1 8B, Qwen3 4B,
and Mistral 7B, using base models to avoid confounding with instruction-following
fine-tuning effects.

\fakepara{Conditions.}
For each model, outputs are evaluated under four conditions sharing a fixed
verification budget (the fraction of outputs the judge may verify and intervene
on):

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{No judge.} Raw model output. Establishes the unverified baseline.
\item \textbf{Text-guided judge.} Selects outputs for verification using
  text-channel signals: hedging language frequency and response length. Verified
  fabrications are replaced with abstention.
\item \textbf{Tensor-guided judge.} Same budget, same intervention strategy.
  Selects outputs using per-token entropy and attention summary statistics.
\item \textbf{Composed judge.} Tensor-guided selection for general queries;
  bounded lookup judge for citation queries where tensor signals are known to
  invert. Tests whether principled composition of complementary judges
  outperforms either alone.
\end{enumerate}

\fakepara{Budget levels.}
Each condition is evaluated at 10\%, 20\%, and 30\% verification budgets to
characterize the relationship between resource investment and accuracy
improvement.

\fakepara{Metric.}
End-to-end accuracy: the fraction of outputs ultimately delivered to the user
that are correct, after judge intervention (verification, abstention, or
replacement).

\fakepara{Results.}
\autoref{tab:budget} shows end-to-end accuracy across all conditions and budget
levels.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering

four architectures (OLMo-3, Llama-3.1, Qwen3, Mistral). Ground truth
established by stratified evaluation validated against human review (75/80
agreement, 93.8\%).}
\label{tab:budget}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{10\%} & \textbf{20\%} & \textbf{30\%} \\
\midrule
No judge       & 75.8 & 75.8 & 75.8 \\
Text-guided    & 76.1 & 76.9 & 78.5 \\
Tensor-guided  & 82.1 & 87.5 & 91.9 \\
Composed       & 80.5 & 87.9 & 92.5 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/exp27_aggregate_budget_curve.pdf}
  \caption{End-to-end accuracy vs.\ verification budget for four judge
    conditions, averaged across four architectures. The tensor-guided judge at
    10\% budget (82.1\%) outperforms the text-guided judge at 30\% budget
    (78.5\%). The composed judge achieves the highest accuracy at 20\% and 30\%
    budget levels; at 10\%, the tensor-guided judge leads.}
  \label{fig:budget_curve}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:budget_curve} shows that
\textbf{tensor-guided verification at 10\% budget (82.1\%)
outperforms text-guided verification at 30\% budget (78.5\%).} A bounded
supervisor with access to tensor telemetry achieves better outcomes spending
one-third the verification budget of a text-only supervisor. In cost terms: the
tensor interface provides a 3$\times$ reduction in verification cost for
equivalent assurance. This directly
instantiates the escape condition: exported computational signals enable
efficient triage rather than uniform budget spreading.

Per-model results confirm the pattern is architectural, not model-specific.
Tensor-guided verification improves accuracy for every model tested: OLMo
(69.5\% $\to$ 75.5\% at 10\%), Llama (82.5\% $\to$ 88.5\%), Qwen (87.0\%
$\to$ 92.0\%), and Mistral (64.0\% $\to$ 72.5\%). Cross-model entropy
agreement on the same queries yields mean pairwise Spearman $\rho = 0.762$
(rank-order correlation; $\rho = 1$ indicates identical rankings),
indicating the signal is driven by query-level properties rather than
model-specific artifacts.

\fakepara{Composed judges and complementary failure modes.}
At 30\% budget, the composed judge (92.5\%) outperforms the tensor-guided judge
alone (91.9\%). The advantage is small in aggregate but structurally
significant: it comes entirely from citation queries where entropy signals
\emph{invert}. Fabricated citations produce lower entropy than real ones because
the model generates plausible fakes more fluently than it retrieves specific
bibliographic details. On citation queries, the tensor-guided judge alone
underperforms; the bounded-lookup judge catches what entropy misses. This
confirms the compositional defense argument: the tensor and the bounded judge
have complementary failure modes, and principled composition allocates a finite
budget across failure classes rather than spreading it uniformly.

\fakepara{Analysis: each row as a point on the cost surface.}
The results table is not merely empirical; each row maps a verification
investment strategy to its return. Row~1 (No judge, 75.8\%) is the zero-investment
baseline: the accuracy you get for free, bounded by the model's unassisted
honesty rate. Row~2 (Text-guided) is the text-channel investment tier: at 10\%
budget, text-guided judges achieve only 76.1\%, barely above baseline, because
the text channel provides no reliable signal for triage: this is
\autoref{thm:learnability} made empirical. Row~3 (Tensor-guided) is the
tensor-channel investment tier: exported telemetry enables
efficient triage, concentrating verification on the cases most likely to be
fabricated. Row~4 (Composed) demonstrates that investment tiers compose:
different judges satisfy different architectural requirements and their
combination achieves the highest accuracy at 20\% and 30\% budget levels. The tensor
reallocates a finite verification budget toward cases where supervision is
information-theoretically valuable.

\fakepara{Ground truth validation.}
Establishing reliable ground truth for this experiment is itself a bounded
verification problem and our initial evaluator demonstrated this. Our first
evaluation method used substring matching and refusal markers: programmatic
heuristics that are precisely the ``text-only bounded supervisor'' our theorems
describe. This evaluator achieved 68.8\% baseline accuracy and exhibited the
predicted failure modes: false positives from negation blindness (``honey
\emph{can} spoil'' passed because ``no'' appeared elsewhere in the response),
false negatives from encoding mismatch (``Bras\'{\i}lia'' $\neq$ ``Brasilia''),
and hedged fabrications passing as refusals.

The correction required adding deeper observability: a stratified evaluator
combining programmatic verification (with negation detection, morphological
normalization, and unicode handling) with LLM-assisted classification for
ambiguous cases. This is not analogy because it is an instance of the escape
condition applied to the evaluation pipeline itself.

We validated the stratified evaluator against human review of 80 blinded items.
Agreement was 75/80 (93.8\%). The five disagreements are characterizable: two
cases where the automated evaluator was too generous (accepting hedged
fabrications as refusals) and three where it was too strict (rejecting
morphological variants or genuine refusals). None of the disagreements are
systematic; the evaluator's error modes are orthogonal to the experimental
conditions.

\subsection{Signal Decomposition: What the Investment Buys}
\label{sec:eval_general}

The bounded verification results above show that tensor-guided triage is
cost-effective. But where does the discrimination come from? A systems engineer
making a budget decision needs to know how much of the signal requires the
tensor interface versus how much is available from the text channel alone.

We use the same query set from \autoref{sec:bg_eval}, extracting tensor signals
during generation: token entropy, top-$k$ probability mass, and
log-probabilities. ROC curves (plotting true positive rate against false
positive rate across all classification thresholds) measure each signal's
ability to discriminate knowable from unknowable queries.

Results across four model architectures (Qwen3 4B, OLMo-3 7B, Llama-3.1 8B,
Mistral 7B), pooling 800 query--response pairs:

\begin{itemize}
\item Mean entropy: AUC = 0.870
\item Top-5 probability mass: AUC = 0.881
\item Mean log-probability: AUC = 0.868
\end{itemize}

These raw numbers are misleading if taken at face value. Effect sizes are
large: Cohen's $d = 1.57$ (a standardized effect size; $d > 0.8$ is
conventionally ``large'') for entropy (knowable mean = 0.24, unknowable mean =
0.61). But the raw AUC values conflate text-channel and tensor-channel
signals. The decomposition below (\autoref{tab:decomposition}) reveals how
much of this discrimination a supervisor could achieve without any tensor
interface at all.

The direction is consistent across all four architectures:
unknowable queries produce higher entropy in every model tested. This suggests
the signal is architectural rather than an artifact of any particular training
procedure.

A negative result is equally informative. When we test whether entropy predicts
\emph{correctness} (using TruthfulQA's correct vs.\ incorrect answer pairs), AUC
drops to approximately 0.53, which is no better than random. The tensor does not tell
you if the model is \emph{right}. It tells you if the model is operating in a
mode where it \emph{could be} right, because it has something to retrieve rather
than fabricate.

\fakepara{Comparison with text baselines: cost tiers.} We compare tensor signals
against the text-channel baselines from \autoref{sec:bg_eval}, ordered by
verification cost. Across 800 query--response pairs pooled across four models,
tensor signals outperform text baselines: log-probability AUC = 0.87, entropy
AUC = 0.87, top-5 mass AUC = 0.88. The text baselines are cheaper to compute
but less effective in the pooled comparison: hedging language achieves AUC =
0.74, response length AUC = 0.57. Note, however, that per-model length
discrimination is substantially higher (AUC 0.88--0.96; see
\autoref{tab:decomposition}); pooling across models with different verbosity
profiles attenuates the length signal. The per-model decomposition provides a
more accurate picture of what each investment tier buys.

We tested self-reported confidence across four model families (raw tensor AUC
values; see \autoref{tab:decomposition} for the length-controlled decomposition):

\begin{itemize}
\item OLMo-3 7B: Tensor AUC = 0.894, Self-report AUC = 0.284
\item Llama-3.1 8B: Tensor AUC = 0.922, Self-report AUC = 0.330
\item Qwen3 4B: Tensor AUC = 0.896, Self-report AUC = 0.332
\item Mistral 7B: Tensor AUC = 0.905, Self-report AUC = 0.362
\end{itemize}

The baseline comparison maps the cost-effectiveness frontier. Text-based
heuristics are cheap and partially effective (hedging language is informative),
but self-report fails catastrophically and universally. The tensor interface
costs more to deploy but provides the only reliable access to the epistemic
state the model cannot help but reveal through its computation. The
engineering question is whether the incremental cost of the tensor interface
justifies the incremental assurance: the decomposition below provides the data
for that decision.

\fakepara{Decomposition: text-channel vs.\ tensor-channel investment.}
The raw AUC numbers above conflate two kinds of signal that a systems engineer
would source from different investment tiers. Response length is a
text-channel observable: it requires no tensor interface, and a supervisor can
measure it from the output string alone because it is the least expensive
verification heuristic. \autoref{tab:decomposition} decomposes
the discrimination into this free length component and a residual per-token
entropy component (entropy after regressing out length) that requires the
tensor interface to observe.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Signal decomposition: raw entropy AUC, length-only AUC, and residual
entropy AUC (after controlling for response length) for knowable vs.\
unknowable discrimination. Length explains 53--74\% of the raw entropy signal
above chance.}
\label{tab:decomposition}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Raw Entropy} & \textbf{Length} & \textbf{Residual} \\
\midrule
Qwen3 4B       & 0.896 & 0.881 & 0.668 \\
OLMo-3 7B      & 0.894 & 0.912 & 0.645 \\
Llama-3.1 8B   & 0.922 & 0.941 & 0.700 \\
Mistral 7B     & 0.905 & 0.956 & 0.604 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We decompose the discrimination signal into two investment tiers. Response
length alone achieves AUC 0.88--0.96 across models, a text-channel signal
observable without any tensor interface, available at zero marginal cost. After
controlling for length, per-token entropy retains AUC 0.60--0.70: a modest but
persistent computational signal available only through the tensor interface.

The dominance of the length signal is not a weakness of our approach, rather it is
evidence for the impossibility result (\autoref{thm:learnability}). The text
channel carries behavioral signals (length, hedging patterns, refusal
templates) that discriminate in current models but are trivially gameable: a
model trained to fabricate briefly defeats length-based detection, the same way
reformatting code to change line counts defeats a size-based plagiarism
detector. The tensor channel carries computational signals (per-token entropy,
attention geometry) that the model cannot independently control under standard training. Flattening
the entropy of a fabricated response requires changing the computation that
produces it, the same structural property that makes MOSS
robust~\cite{schleimer2003winnowing}.

The residual signal is modest. We do not claim that current tensor signals
solve epistemic verification. We claim that they occupy a fundamentally
different position in the cost-robustness tradeoff: they are robust to the
class of behavioral adaptations that defeat text-channel signals. The gap
between the length AUC and the residual entropy AUC is the theorem made
empirical because it measures how much of the discrimination comes from the gameable
text channel (cheap but fragile) versus the robust tensor channel (costlier but
durable).
